## Key Considerations for This Application

**Acoustically transparent (AT) screens** typically lose 10-20% of light transmission compared to solid screens. This means a projector rated at 2,500 lumens effectively delivers 2,000-2,250 lumens on an AT screen. In a fully dark, dedicated room this is still adequate for a 145" 2.35:1 image (which has roughly the same area as a 120" 16:9 image), but it makes brightness matter more than in a solid-screen setup.

**145" 2.35:1 screen dimensions:** ~134" wide x 57" tall (image width ~11.2 feet).

**Throw distance math:** For an 11.2-foot-wide image with 11-15 feet of throw distance, the required throw ratio is approximately 1.0:1 to 1.34:1. This is on the short side and eliminates some projectors or requires them to use the wide end of their zoom range (where image quality can be slightly reduced).

## Ultra Short Throw (UST) Projectors

**Note:** UST projectors are generally **not recommended** for use with acoustically transparent screens. AT screens are typically stretched on a frame with speakers behind them, and UST projectors need to be placed inches from a flat wall-mounted screen. The geometry is fundamentally incompatible. These are included for completeness but are likely not viable for this application.

## Recommendations

### Best Overall for Dedicated Dark Room with AT Screen
**JVC DLA-NZ500 ($5,999) — if budget can stretch $1,000**

The 40,000:1 native contrast ratio is in a completely different class from everything else under $6,000. In a fully dark, light-controlled room, native contrast is the single most important image quality metric — it determines how deep blacks look, how much shadow detail is visible, and how "cinematic" the image feels. The NZ500 adds laser reliability (no lamp replacements) and a throw ratio that works at shorter distances (1.26:1 minimum). Native 4K D-ILA chips also deliver genuinely sharper detail than pixel-shifted 1080p panels.

### Best Within $5,000 Budget
**Epson Pro Cinema LS9000 ($3,999)**

The newest Epson laser delivers the LS12000's 2,500,000:1 dynamic contrast at $1,000 less, with laser reliability, massive lens shift flexibility, and 4K pixel shifting that looks very good in practice. The slightly lower brightness vs the LS12000 (2,200 vs 2,700 lumens) is the main trade-off, but it remains adequate for a 145" AT screen in a dark room. The $1,000 savings vs the LS12000 can go toward a better AT screen, which will have more impact on final image quality than the marginal brightness difference.

If brightness is a concern with the AT screen, step up to the **Epson Pro Cinema LS12000 ($4,999)** for 2,700 lumens and wider horizontal lens shift at the budget ceiling.

### Best Value
**Epson Home Cinema LS11000 ($3,999)**

Same price as the LS9000 with 300 more lumens (2,500 vs 2,200) and wider lens shift. The lower dynamic contrast (1,200,000:1 vs 2,500,000:1) is a trade-off, but in practice the difference is subtle. If you can still find one in stock, it is an excellent value. However, availability is becoming limited as it ages out.

### Best if Budget Can Stretch
**JVC DLA-NZ500 ($5,999)**

Reiterating: if the extra $1,000 is feasible, the NZ500 is the projector to buy. Native 4K, native 40,000:1 contrast, laser light source, compact form factor, and JVC's industry-leading dark room image quality. Nothing else under $10,000 matches it for a dedicated dark theater.

## Notes on AT Screen Selection

The projector choice and AT screen are interdependent decisions. Key considerations:
- **Weave density** affects light loss: tighter weaves lose more light (15-20%) but have less visible texture; looser weaves lose less (8-12%) but may show moiré patterns
- **Screen gain** can partially compensate for AT light loss — a 1.1-1.3 gain AT screen recovers some brightness
- **Seymour AV, Stewart, and Screen Innovations** are leading AT screen manufacturers for dedicated theaters
- A lower-brightness projector (like the JVC at 2,000 lm) benefits more from a higher-gain, lower-loss AT screen
- Budget the screen at $1,500-3,000 for a quality 145" 2.35:1 AT panel
